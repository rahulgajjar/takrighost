About Takri

May 19, 2021 5 min read

The origin of the name Takri is uncertain. It is also known as Takari, Takkari, Tankri, etc. It is also believed that it comes from the old landed class of Punjab called Takka, or that it is a script of Takkas.

Takri alphabets are related to Sharda and Gurmukhi alphabets. It is descended from the Devāśeṣa alphabet, which developed from the Sharada alphabet. The Takri script was in use between the 16th and 19th centuries. It was mainly used in the region now known as  Jammu, Himachal Pradesh, Pahari region of Punjab, and Uttarakhand. Different variations of Takri script were used to write different languages like Chambeali, Dogri, and other Pahari languages.

Takri is written from left to right using thirty-four consonant letters, ten vowel letters, and ten vowel diacritics. It is an abugida. (abugida means without the use of vowel signs) It uses dynamic letters which are written depending on the form of the consonant. Each consonant carries an inherent ‘a’ vowel. A vowel sign is attached to change the inherent vowel. Other vowels are written with one of the vowel diacritics unless they appear at the beginning of a word. In that case, they are written using one of the independent vowel letters. The script structure is much like Devanagari. Since Takri fell out of use, people use Devanagari to write languages like Chambeali or Dogri. One notable feature of Takri is that most of its variants do not use headstroke.

Takri has a significant variation in style, it was used in the wide mountainous area and prone to many regional variants developed in isolated mountain communities. That is the main reason why it has significant variations in style. The communities standardized the form of the script which they themselves used but there was no universally applicable standardized variety. However, despite variations in the shapes of letters and the styles of writing, each form was recognized by its users to be Takri, and each variety exhibited certain common features.

Takri: Script and language

Let’s first differentiate between script, language, and dialect. Language is a method of communication, usually refers as the spoken language. While script refers to a collection of symbols (characters) that can be used to write one or more language. Dialect is the variation of the same language.

Takri script has its origin in the Sharda family of Brahmi script. It is also related to Gurmukhi and Landa. Takri is descended from Sharda through an intermediate form known as Devasesa (Devāśeṣa) which was emerged in the 14th century. Devasesa was used for religious and official purposes while Takri was used for commercial and informal purposes. Takri became differentiated from Devasesa during the 16th century. Takri served as the official script of several states of northern India from the 17th century until the mid 20th century. A reformed version of the Takri used for writing Chambeali was established as the official script of the former Chamba state. A variation of Takri was adopted as the official script of Jammu and Kashmir and it was standardized as Dogra Akkhar.

Because Takri was the official and popular script, it appears in various records from manuscripts to inscriptions. Takri was used for writing administrative documents, letters, land grants, official decree, etc. During that time metal type for Takri was developed and it was used to print translations of Christain religious texts into the Chambeli language in Takri. Other sources for Takri records are inscriptions on Pahari paintings.

There were many variants of the Takri script like Bhattiyali, Chambeali, Gaddi, Jansuri, Gahri Kangri, Kinnauri, Kishtwari, Kulli, Mandeali, Sirmauri, etc. There is no universal standard form of Takri, in cases where Takri was standardized, it was limited to a particular policy. The Takri samples found in Grierson’s Linguistic Survey of India also state that the author choose the representative forms of the Takri characters and it should not be considered as the standard form of Takri as it may vary across different localities. Takri could be considered as more of a group of scripts rather than a specific script.

Script structure

The underlying structure of writing Takri is similar to Devanagari or Gurmukhi. Most of the writing found in Takri is without the joining of Shirorekha (Shirorekha is the line made on the upper part of the word in a script like Devanagari) Takri is an abugida, the orthography of the script reflects the pronunciation of the language. It is written from left to right. Takri has 33 consonants and 10 vowels. Consonant letters have an inherent vowel sound. Combining vowel signs are attached to the consonant to indicate that a different vowel follows the consonant.

Takri orthography is based on orthographic syllables: a syllable includes clusters of consonants without intervening vowel sound plus any following vowels and diacritics. Consonant clusters are typically represented as partially merged forms, called conjuncts. The very older forms of Takri had a large number of conjuncts, but the modern form has a very simplified approach to the conjuncts. When consonants except for Ha, Ya, Ra are partially merged with Ha, Ya or Ra, it forms a sign which is attached below the letter or after the letter. The half forms of consonants are not written explicitly and they are meant to be understood by users based on the context.
